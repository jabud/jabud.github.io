Equation Of a Line


A line, such simple figure with so many use cases. A line is the simplest method to explain something, to draw conclusions (or disappointing cartoons), to mark a trend or classify groups.

We even have a mathematical category called linear algebra to celebrate lines. This category is central in mathematics and used everywhere, geometry, engineering, physics, statistics and particularly… machine learning.

Linear algebra is represented by matrices and vector spaces that allow us to manipulate quantities and shapes, translate them, rotate them, squeeze them or expand them as we please… and it all comes down to a simple equation:



I wasn’t planning on writing a whole article on this expression but it seems like an important thing to do since this equation is used by many methods in the realm of machine learning, so I wrote it and I will refer you to it in the future as needed.

Let’s begin with Sigma ∑, (or as I like to call it: the weird E) represents a summation of the expression evaluated on each value of xi, where i stands for each instance of our data. The extended version looks like this:



Each section inside a parenthesis is a point of our line.

X represents values in the x axis, usually its values are known, for instance, they could be the days in which we registered and observed some piece of data, or the height of people in centimeters.

If we substitute the x values, let’s say from 120 cms. to 200 cms. and leave m and b constant, say equal to 1, we can gradually create a line, as follows:
